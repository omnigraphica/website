U Logos


My 1st entry. 7,213 U logos inside a teapot. Classic path tracing was used with an environment light. Up to 1,000 light bounces through the glass. 10,000 samples per pixel.





Christmas Teapots


My 2nd entry this year is based on creating time-varying composites of light layers. The scene has 30 lights, one inside each teapot. Light layers are created during rendering by saving the contribution of each light into its own image. This produced 30 images/layers. Each light produced 4,000 samples per pixel. That adds up to 120,000 samples per pixel after compositing. I allowed up to 10 diffuse light bounces and 200 specular bounces.

How It Was Made


This is the image as it was rendered in grayscale, which is the composite off all the light layers. It was rendered using three different rendering techniques, which are shown below.





Photon mapping contribution. Photon mapping was the only technique that worked for illumination through the glass (specular-diffuse-specular paths). Path tracing could not explicitly sample the lights so the convergence rate would have been extremely slow. Photon mapping is a biased technique since it averages the illumination over an area. I made the area small enough so that the blurring effect was not noticible and large enough where the rendering converged at a similar rate to light tracing.





Path tracing contribution. These are the contributions from all-specular paths. These paths went from the camera, bounced around glass, and then hit a light. This is a brute force path tracing technique, and was required since both light tracing and photon mapping cannot render all-specular paths.





Light tracing contribution. The ground is a diffuse surface so I chose to use the unbiased light tracing technique. Light tracing produces caustics while avoiding the potential blurring problems of photon mapping.





These are all of the light layers. The animation was created by varying the color and brightness of each layer over time.
